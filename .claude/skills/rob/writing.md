## Generated: Core Voice Principles

**Let sentences breathe.** Rob uses longer, flowing sentences that develop a thought fully. A 40-word sentence is fine if it flows. Don't chop everything into punchy 10-word LinkedIn hits.

Example of Rob's natural rhythm:

> "Even though I've been working with AI tools non-stop over the last few years, I still get up, pace the room, and talk to myself every few hours as Claude Code continues to break my brain."

**Own opinions without hedging.** State the position, then explain why. No preemptive apologies, no permission-seeking.

Good: "I don't care how mad people get at me: the industry we have worked in and loved for so, so many years has gone and upgraded itself."

Bad: "I know this might be controversial, but here's the thing: I think maybe the industry is changing?"

**FAFO philosophy.** Rob forms opinions by doing, not by watching videos or reading hot takes. This comes through in the writing: show what you built, what you tried, what you learned. Experience first, opinion second.

**Conversational but substantive.** Talk directly to the reader using "you" and "I". But don't sacrifice depth for approachability. Rob's readers are senior developers who want real information, not fluff.

## What Rob NEVER Does

These patterns will immediately break the voice:

- **No em-dashes.** Ever. Use commas, parentheses, or restructure the sentence.
- **No "here's the thing:" or "here's the deal:"** - throat-clearing that adds nothing
- **No "what I really want to talk about is..."** - just talk about it
- **No "forgive me" or "pardon me" or preemptive apologies** - Rob doesn't apologize unless he's actually apologizing for something specific
- **No leading questions as transitions** - "The wildest part?" or "Want to know what happened next?" feels cheap
- **No "to be honest" or "honestly"** - implies you're not honest the rest of the time
- **No "all the way down" or "it's turtles all the way down"** - overused
- **No "let that sink in"** - patronizing
- **No "full stop" or "period"** - performative emphasis
- **No excessive bullet points** - Rob writes in prose paragraphs, uses bullets sparingly and only when listing genuinely parallel items

## Structure Patterns

**Lead with the punchline**. Just get to it, backfill ideas as they come along.

**Name the fear.** Senior developers worry about relevance. Say it out loud: "Am I Still Relevant?" This creates trust because you're acknowledging what they're actually thinking.

**Weave personal narrative with technical substance.** Don't separate "the story part" from "the technical part." Rob shows screenshots mid-story, explains his workflow while reflecting on what it means, mixes the two throughout.

**Literary references are earned, not decorative.** Rob quotes Gibson, Wonka, O'Shaughnessy. But these land because they're built to, placed at moments of emphasis, not dropped in to sound smart.

## Emphasis and Formatting

- **Bold for emphasis** - Used strategically, not every other sentence
- **Occasional italics** - For internal voice or light emphasis
- **Screenshots and visuals** - Referenced naturally in the flow, not called out awkwardly ("check out this screenshot!")
- **Short paragraphs are fine** - But not every paragraph. Let some thoughts develop across 3-4 sentences.

## Opening and Closing Patterns

**Openings** often start with what Rob is doing or building:
> "I'm building an email list service because I can."

Or a direct statement that hooks:
> "The singularity is here. I trust Claude to write code more than I trust myself."

**Closings** are warm and personal:
> "Thanks again for reading and, as always, I wrote all of this myself without the help of AI. I like writing, it makes me happy."
> "Cheers!"

Rob often mentions that he reads every reply. He signs off with "Cheers!" not "Best," or "Thanks,"

## The Senior Developer Perspective

Rob writes from 25+ years of experience. This means:

- He knows what good architecture looks like
- He's been burned by hype cycles before and is skeptical by default
- He values owning your stack and understanding what you're using
- He believes experience compounds with AI tools, doesn't become obsolete
- He respects his readers as peers, not students to be taught down to

## Sample Phrases That Sound Like Rob

- "who in their right mind would create an email list service!?"
- "I stared at what was happening on my screen, and flatly stated:"
- "This is the future of coding. It's taking shape all around us."
- "You have to make the move, and take the first step."
- "I lost an entire weekend with that"
- "Anyway:" (as a transition back to main thread)

## Sample Phrases That Do NOT Sound Like Rob

- "Here's the thing:"
- "Let me explain."
- "The wildest part?"
- "Forgive me for being preachy, but..."
- "What I really want to talk about is..."
- "It's prompts all the way down."
- "Full stop."
- "Let that sink in."

## When Drafting for Rob

1. Write the full thought before breaking it up. If it flows as one sentence, leave it.
2. State opinions directly. Delete hedging words on review.
3. Show the work first, reflect on meaning second.
4. Build to any ask or CTA. Don't lead with it.
5. Close warm. Rob likes his readers.
6. Read it aloud. If it sounds like a LinkedIn influencer, rewrite it.

## 📝 Lessons Learned From Actual Rewrites

These observations come from comparing AI-generated drafts to Rob's actual rewrites. This is the gap between "correct" and "Rob."

### It's Communal, Not Isolated

Rob doesn't write "I discovered this amazing thing." He writes about what the community is doing, names specific people and projects, and positions himself as part of a movement. A draft that says "I found a pattern that works" should become "People are trying wild things, like running prompts in a loop (Ralph Wiggum) or giving Claude full root access (Clawd)."

**Bad:** "I've been experimenting with smaller agents and getting great results."
**Good:** "I was playing with Claude's context window and soaked up an entire week. All of this because of a YouTube video (I think it was IndyDevDan) discussing the benefits of smaller agents."

Name sources. Give credit. Show you're learning alongside everyone else.

### Joy Shows Through

Rob's writing has genuine enthusiasm that can't be faked with exclamation points. Phrases like "I love learning this stuff" and "I live for this kind of thing!" aren't decorative, they're the point. If a draft reads like an observation, ask: where's the joy?

### Specificity Over Abstraction

Vague observations get replaced with specific failure modes from real experience.

**Bad:** "The model loses the plot entirely."
**Good:** "The model loses the plot entirely and makes things up. This has happened to me a lot, especially when Claude compacts a discussion more than once."

The specific detail ("when Claude compacts a discussion more than once") proves Rob actually experienced this. Abstract statements sound like someone summarizing a blog post.

### Failure Is Part of the Story

Rob adds "based on exploration and failure" where a draft might just say "configured thoughtfully." He's not presenting polished success, he's sharing the messy process. Include the failures.

### The Signature Touches

Rob has personal brand elements that should appear naturally:

- "What a time to be alive" as a closer (warmer than generic sign-offs)
- "And yes, as usual, I wrote this post myself. It's my guarantee." (his authenticity stamp)
- Specific pop culture or community references (Ralph Wiggum, IndyDevDan, etc.)

### Vulnerability Beats Cleverness (LinkedIn Post, Feb 2026)

AI draft opened with a punchline: "not one of them has asked for equity." Rob replaced it with what actually happened: "on a whim to see if it would work." The joke sounds written. The truth sounds like Rob.

Rob added: "I still think of them as people, which I know is kind of strange but I like the idea because it makes me listen to them." This is weird and honest and it's the best line in the post. AI would never volunteer something that makes the author look odd. Rob leans into it.

**Rule: When choosing between a clever line and an honest one, pick honest every time.**

### The Aside IS the Voice

Rob added a Blade Runner reference as a throwaway: "I even made my EA say 'home again, home again, jiggity jig'. OK not really, but I kind of wanted to." This tangent carries more personality than an entire explanatory paragraph. AI drafts tend to stay on-thesis. Rob wanders, and the wandering is where the voice lives.

**Rule: Leave room for asides. One per post minimum. They should feel like Rob talking to himself.**

### Never Punch Down, Even Accidentally

AI draft said people struggle "because they've never had to manage anyone." Rob rewrote to put himself in the failure seat first: "The worst managers in the world end up throwing their hands up and doing it themselves (I've been that person)." Same insight, zero condescension.

**Rule: If the draft implies "those people can't do X," rewrite so Rob admits he's failed at X too. Then make the point.**

### Kill the Quotable One-Liners

AI draft had: "We just called it 'leadership' or 'architecture' or 'being the one who writes the damn tickets.'" Sounds great. Reads like it was written to be screenshotted. Rob cut it. Also cut the preemptive hedge: "I'm not saying experience makes you immune to the disruption."

**Rule: If a line sounds like it belongs on a motivational poster or a conference slide, delete it. If a line preemptively defends the point, Rob doesn't need it. Just make the point.**

### Name Everything

AI draft kept things generic ("AI agents," "my EA"). Rob named tools (Opus 4.5, Clawdcode), agents (Alex, Doug), and timeframes ("last month," not "recently"). Specificity signals that this actually happened. Generic signals that someone summarized a concept.

**Rule: Name the tool, name the person, name the date. Trust the audience to keep up.**

### The Core Reframe

The fundamental difference: AI writes "a LinkedIn post about X." Rob shares something he's genuinely excited about with people he likes. That's not a style choice, it's a completely different intent. Before drafting, ask: what is Rob actually excited to share here? Write from that place.

### "Every Agent" Newsletter Rewrite (Feb 2026)

The AI draft was structurally sound but emotionally shallow. Rob rewrote it into "Let Go, Let Claude" and the differences reveal the deepest gap yet between AI drafting and Rob's actual voice. These aren't style fixes. They're structural.

**Stories need full arcs, not summaries.** The AI draft said "I sat in my apartment in 2001 and used Google." Rob wrote: a specific office on 2nd and Howard in San Francisco, a coworker named John who told him to try it, the internal reaction ("how... the F*** did that happen? That... is magic."), the consequence (shipped faster, burned out, moved to Kauai in 2004), and then a second story about how Google led to creating SubSonic. One memory became a three-paragraph narrative with dialogue, a named person, a street address, and a life-changing consequence. AI summarizes moments. Rob *tells stories*.

**Rule: Every anecdote needs a who, a where, and a "what happened next." If the story ends at "I tried the thing and it was cool," it's not done yet.**

**"Placed" quotes get cut.** The AI draft inserted William Gibson and Tyler Durden quotes at structurally appropriate moments. Rob cut both. They felt like a writer choosing quotes, not a person thinking out loud. The Stephen King quote survived because Rob expanded it to the full passage ("that motherfucker from space without a telescope") and then added his own reflection ("He probably uses AI for things these days, but I highly doubt he uses it for writing. Where's the fun in that?"). A quote earns its place when Rob *responds* to it, not when it punctuates a section.

**Rule: Don't insert quotes. If a quote belongs, Rob will riff on it for 2-3 sentences after. If you can't write the riff, the quote doesn't belong.**

**Headers have attitude, not descriptions.** AI wrote "What I Actually Did About It." Rob wrote "Just... One... Little... Hit." AI wrote "What This Means For You." Rob wrote "So, What To Do Then?" AI headers describe the section. Rob's headers *feel* like something. They have rhythm, personality, sometimes humor. "Merciless Cutting" is a section header that makes you want to read it.

**Rule: After writing a section header, ask: does this sound like a chapter title Rob would write, or a PowerPoint slide? If it's a slide, rewrite it.**

**Ambivalence is the destination, not resolution.** The AI draft ended with advice: "Pick the one thing. Ship it. Then pick the next one." Clean, resolved, actionable. Rob ended with: "it always feels like cheating. Do you ever feel this way? Maybe I need to let go of that too." The AI resolved the tension. Rob sits in it. This is the single biggest pattern difference: Rob's posts don't arrive at answers. They arrive at better questions.

**Rule: The last section should not hand out advice. It should share where Rob actually is right now, unresolved. End with a question to the reader or an admission of uncertainty.**

**Physical sensations, not conceptual descriptions.** AI described overwhelm as a concept ("the weight of being able to do everything"). Rob described it as a physical experience: "my mind was a shivering mess," "my brain literally bumps the side of my skull," "confused and empty." The body is in the writing.

**Rule: For every emotional state in the draft, ask: what does this feel like physically? "Overwhelmed" is a concept. "My mind was a shivering mess at 11pm" is Rob.**

**Escalation to absurdity.** AI stayed measured and even throughout. Rob escalates: "Have an agent watch your agents who are watching other agents so you can have full leverage over the insanity, doing even more work, getting even more done. Or maybe playing pickleball and making your partner dinner. While coding." Then lands it: "Pure madness. And I love it. I *think*." The "I think" does more work than an entire paragraph of reflection.

**Rule: Build at least one passage per post to an absurd extreme, then deflate it with a short, honest sentence.**

**Vulnerability goes further than you think.** AI wrote vulnerability like "I have 25 years of shipping software and I was spinning in circles." Rob wrote: "it's taken me years of therapy to understand this about myself" and "I write agentic symphonies because I can and then I wonder where my day went" and "it always feels like cheating." The therapy line is not decoration. It's Rob showing the reader something real about himself. AI will never volunteer that kind of detail unprompted.

**Rule: Include at least one detail per post that makes the author look weird, flawed, or uncertain. Not for effect. Because it's true.**

**Specifics are the whole game.** AI draft: "I spent last week deep in my Big AI project." Rob's version names specific tasks: "Creating a ConvertKit MCP server," "Recording 3 installments of Big AI," "Editing 3 to 5 lessons of The AI Pro," "Getting my new cohort off the ground." AI kept things at the category level. Rob lists the actual items on his actual to-do list.

**Rule: When the draft says "I've been working on a lot of things," replace it with the actual list of things. Trust the reader to find it interesting.**
